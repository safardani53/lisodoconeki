    18 psychiatric patients released by the same NHS trust all went on to KILL

    AN NHS Trust is under pressure after a damning investigation found 18 released psychiatric patients all went on to kill. Their victims include an ex-midwife who was strangled by her daughter and a father who was beaten to death following an argument in a pub.

    27 Feb 2024, 00:00

    AN NHS Trust is under pressure after a damning investigation found 18 released psychiatric patients all went on to kill.

    Their victims include an ex-midwife who was strangled by her daughter and a father who was beaten to death following an argument in a pub.

    The situation at Oxleas NHS Foundation Trust in south-east London may be part of a wider problem of patients being discharged "too hastily", according to the Daily Mail investigation.

    As many as 835 people have been killed by people with mental health issues between 2005 and 2015, a government commissioned report found.

    The true number could be much higher, says Julian Hendy of the Hundred Families charity who uncovered the Oxleas cases through a series of Freedom of Information requests.

    Hendy, whose own father was stabbed to death by a psychotic patient, founded the charity on the concept that around 100 families will lose a relative to someone with mental illness every year.

    He said: "This figure [18] is far too high and the number is not decreasing, so there is no evidence that Oxleas is learning any lessons from these appalling tragedies."

    What is more worrying, the Mail claims, is these killings took place in just one area of the country.

    Responding to the allegations, Oxleas issued a statement saying "all serious incidents involving our patients are fully investigated to identify any improvements in care".

    It told the Mail: "In addition, independent inquiries are held if a homicide is carried out by a person with mental illness under our care."

    "Where possible, family members of the victim are involved in the inquiry... Each suicide or homicide is a personal tragedy and we know the devastating impact they can have on families and communities."

    Sun Online tried to contact the under-fire Trust but they could not provide a spokesperson over the weekend.

    One of the patients released from Oxleas, which provides specialist care at 125 sites in the capital, is Daniel Atkins.

    He beat his neighbour Ronald Parsons, 71, to death at his flat in Bromley, south-east London two days after he was discharged from Queen Elizabeth Hospital, Woolwich.

    Daniel was diagnosed with a schizo-affective disorder but later became a "poster boy" for the Trust as an example of a mental health patient who could function healthily in society.

    But the Mail reports Atkins had himself told hospital staff that he was not ready for life in the outside world - and his mother reportedly said he was too sick to be discharged.

    It is understood that an internal investigation by Oxleas found that the killing of Parsons "could not have been predicted".

    Another victim, retired midwife Priscilla Edward, was strangled to death by her 53-year-old daughter Regina Edwards in 2016.

    Edwards, 53, who pleaded guilty to manslaughter on ground of diminished responsibility, also suffered from schizo-affective disorder.

    And in 2011, Nicola Edgington tried to decapitate grandmother Sally Hodkin with a meat cleaver in Bexleyheath after she was allowed to leave the Trust's Bracton branch.

    These cases at Oxleas come to light amid anger over the release of "Black Cab rapist" John Worboys.

    Psychologist Jackie Craissati, whose report allegedly contributed to his release, is believed to have worked for Oxleas.

    She was based at its Bracton Centre from 1988 and at the helm as clinical director from 2010 to 2016.

    In 1999, the Journal of Forensic Psychiatry found that two-thirds of schizophrenia patients at Bracton with — 41 out of 63 — went on to re-offend within ten years of being discharged.
