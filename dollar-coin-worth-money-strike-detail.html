    SILVER dollars are coveted by coin collectors, but some are worth more than others.

    After 1935, America stopped producing large-sized silver coins.

    In 1971 the coin made a comeback as the San Fransisco mint produced around $6.8million "Ike" dollars.

    But these aren't the coins collectors are after.

    Rather, they're looking for prototypes.

    A coin collector on TikTok who goes by TreasureTown explained what makes the prototypes so much rarer than the actual coins.

    read more on rare coins

    In the video, he showed one of these coins that sold for $264,000 in January 2022.

    However, that specific coin had a high grade of 67. The highest grade a coin can get is a 70, so this one was near perfect.

    WHAT TO LOOK FOR

    The prototypes weren't discovered until 2008 despite multiple differences, including a different finish.

    They feature an array of other unusual marks like unfinished burring on the lettering, heavy die polishing marks and doubling, according to the Professional Coin Grading Service (PCGS).

    Most read in Money

    The face of the coin has a side profile of President Dwight Eisenhower and the word "Liberty" across the top.

    "In God We Trust" is printed to the right of Eisenhower.

    The reverse shows an eagle on the moon with earth in the background.

    Experts say there are only three known to exist.

    MORE VALUABLE DOLLARS

    After the Coinage Act of 1965, silver was removed from dimes and quarters.

    In the following years, silver was used less and less as nickel and copper took over.

    However, this hasn't stopped silver coins from continuing through circulation since then.

    The Flowing Hair dollar was the first official silver $1 coin issued by the federal government.

    In 2017, one of these circulated coins sold for $910,625 at auction.

    Another one in Mint State (MS) condition sold for $10million in 2013, according to CNN.

    In 1804, the US produced more than 8,000 dimes featuring Lady Liberty on the obverse and an eagle flying under stars on the reverse.

    There were supposed to be 13 stars on the back of the coin, but a small number were minted with 14 instead.

    Circulated 14-star dimes have sold for upwards of $100,000, including one that went for $623,500 2008, according to PCGS.

    A coin doesn't have to be ancient to be valuable, though.
